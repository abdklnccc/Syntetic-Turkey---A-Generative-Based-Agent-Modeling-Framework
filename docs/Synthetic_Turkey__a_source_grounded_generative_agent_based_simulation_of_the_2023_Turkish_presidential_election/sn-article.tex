\title[Synthetic Turkey: GABM voter simulation]{Synthetic Turkey: a source-grounded generative agent-based simulation of the 2023 Turkish presidential election}

\author*[1]{\fnm{Abdullah} \sur{Kılınç}}
\email{abdullahkilinc21@marun.edu.tr}
\affil*[1]{\orgdiv{Department of Management Information Systems},
  \orgname{Marmara University},
  \orgaddress{\city{Istanbul}, \country{Türkiye}}}

\abstract{Generative agent-based modelling (GABM) couples LLM-driven agents
with simulation environments in which agent reasoning, communication, and
decision-making are generated rather than hand-coded. Despite growing interest,
GABM electoral simulation has been applied primarily to two-party or Western
parliamentary settings, leaving polarized multi-party presidential elections
with coalition coordination and strategic voting largely unexplored. This paper
presents \textit{Synthetic Turkey}, a source-grounded GABM of the 2023 Turkish
presidential election, and proposes an evaluation framework that decouples
aggregate accuracy from aggregation sensitivity and failure-mode diagnosis.

The model constructs a deliberately bounded 300-agent synthetic electorate from
12 research-informed voter archetypes anchored in the 2018 Turkish political
baseline. Agents are exposed to a longitudinal 2018--2023 event timeline,
filtered political broadcasts, persistent memory, and election-safe decision
ticks. At each tick, an LLM updates voter beliefs, affect, trust, turnout
probability, candidate probabilities, and a short first-person reflection.

The simulation reproduces broad coalition patterns including loyalist
Erdoğan support, secular and Kurdish opposition consolidation, and
earthquake-zone loyalism. However, the aggregate outcome depends critically
on probability-to-vote conversion rules. Mean-probability aggregation produces
a Kılıçdaroğlu plurality, a deterministic mode-vote rule produces an Erdoğan
victory, and alternative tie-handling rules produce ambiguous results. The
model also under-represents Sinan Oğan's nationalist-protest pathway and shows
damped numerical movement in trust and affect.

Together these findings motivate an evaluation framework for GABM-based
electoral simulations that separates aggregate accuracy from aggregation
sensitivity, archetype-level plausibility, and failure-mode diagnosis. The
paper frames the simulation not as a validated election predictor, but as a
diagnostic method-development study for LLM-mediated electoral simulation.}

\keywords{large language models, generative agent-based modelling,
electoral simulation, voter behaviour simulation, political polarization}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}
% =========================================================================

Large language models (LLMs) have created new possibilities for computational
social science by enabling generative agent-based modelling (GABM), in which
agent cognition, reasoning, memory, communication, or decision-making is partly
generated by an LLM rather than fully specified through hand-coded behavioural
rules \cite{ghaffarzadegan2023gabm,gao2024llmabm,lu2024gabs}. In this paper,
GABM refers to agent-based simulations in which generative AI is embedded
inside the agent decision loop; \textit{Synthetic Turkey} is a source-grounded
GABM of an electoral process, where deterministic code controls the population,
event chronology, information filtering, memory storage, validation, and
aggregation, while the LLM performs voter-level reasoning. Recent work on
generative agents shows that LLM-based agents can store experiences, retrieve
relevant memories, synthesize reflections, and plan behaviour in interactive
environments \cite{park2023generative}. Related work on synthetic respondents
and public-opinion simulation suggests that LLMs can approximate some
survey-like patterns, but also that their outputs require careful validation
and should not be treated as direct substitutes for empirical data
\cite{argyle2023out,aher2023simulating,qu2024performance}. These concerns
become especially important in electoral simulation, where a model may reproduce
a final winner while still failing to capture the mechanisms that produced the
result.

This paper addresses these issues through a source-grounded GABM of the 2023
Turkish presidential election. Türkiye is a difficult case for synthetic
electoral modelling. The 2023 election followed a five-year period marked by
currency crisis, inflation, institutional contestation, opposition alliance
formation, Kurdish party repression and reorganization, the February 2023
earthquakes, nationalist fragmentation, and an unusually competitive presidential
race. The case therefore tests whether LLM-based voter agents can respond not
only to static demographic attributes, but also to a longitudinal sequence of
political events and information environments.

The project, called \textit{Synthetic Turkey}, builds a 300-agent synthetic
electorate from 12 research-informed voter archetypes. Each agent contains a
demographic identity, political worldview, media diet, affective baseline,
behavioural parameters, 2018 electoral memory, and candidate-level vote
intention. Agents are exposed to a 2018--2023 event timeline and to filtered
political broadcasts. At each simulation tick, an LLM acts as the voter brain:
it receives the agent's identity, current state, memories, visible event
information, political messages, and limited social context, then returns
structured updates for trust, approval, emotions, turnout, party preference,
candidate probabilities, and reflection.

The aim of this paper is not to claim that LLM agents can reliably predict
elections. Instead, the study asks a narrower methodological question: what
does a source-grounded GABM electorate reproduce, where does it fail, and what
do those failures reveal about the design of LLM-mediated electoral simulation?
This framing is important because the completed simulation produces both
encouraging and problematic results. The model reproduces several broad
political bloc patterns, but its aggregate election result depends strongly on
how probabilistic candidate intentions are converted into vote shares. Under
mean-probability aggregation, the simulation produces a Kılıçdaroğlu plurality.
Under a deterministic mode-vote rule, the same agent outputs initially produce
an Erdoğan victory. However, further analysis shows that this mode-vote result
is sensitive to top-probability ties: a sizeable minority of agents express tied
maximum probabilities, and alternative tie-handling rules shift the result toward
Kılıçdaroğlu or produce near-indistinguishable candidate shares. This divergence
is treated as a substantive finding rather than a reporting detail.

The study is guided by three research questions:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Can a source-grounded GABM electorate reproduce broad coalition-level
      patterns in the 2023 Turkish presidential election?
\item How sensitive are simulated election outcomes to probability aggregation
      and tie-handling rules?
\item Which voter blocs and behavioural pathways are systematically distorted
      or under-represented by the model?
\end{enumerate}
To answer these questions, the paper evaluates the simulation across five
levels: aggregate vote shares, aggregation and tie-handling sensitivity,
archetype-level voting patterns, demographic diagnostics, and longitudinal state
trajectories. The paper makes three contributions. First, it contributes to
emerging GABM literature by extending LLM-mediated electoral simulation to a
polarized, non-Western, two-round presidential election with coalition
coordination, Kurdish strategic voting, nationalist protest voting,
earthquake-zone retrospective evaluation, and aggregation-sensitive outcomes.
Second, it evaluates the model through a multi-level diagnostic design rather
than a single winner-correctness metric. Third, it diagnoses failure modes that
are likely to matter for future LLM-agent electoral simulations: aggregation
sensitivity, tie-handling sensitivity, missing protest-vote pathways,
over-homogenization of some archetypes, latent LLM knowledge risk, and weak
numerical responsiveness in state variables.

\section{Related work}\label{sec:related}
% =========================================================================

\subsection{From agent-based modelling to generative agent-based
modelling}\label{subsec:abm-to-gabm}

Agent-based modelling has long been used in computational social science to
examine how macro-level social patterns can emerge from micro-level agent
behaviour \cite{epstein2006generative,macal2010tutorial}. Recent work couples
ABM with generative AI, especially LLMs, creating what is increasingly
described as generative agent-based modelling (GABM)
\cite{ghaffarzadegan2023gabm,gao2024llmabm,lu2024gabs}. In GABM, the modeller
no longer specifies every decision rule directly; instead, some agent-level
reasoning, interpretation, communication, or action selection is generated
through natural-language model calls. Park et al.'s generative-agent
architecture introduced agents that store experiences as natural-language
memories, retrieve relevant memories, synthesize reflections, and plan future
behaviour, enabling believable social behaviour in an interactive environment
\cite{park2023generative}.

Synthetic Turkey builds on this GABM and generative-agent tradition but applies
it to a historically bounded electoral simulation rather than an open-ended
social sandbox. The simulation uses persistent voter memory, event exposure,
political broadcasts, and election-safe decision points. This design keeps the
environment, event chronology, and aggregation rules outside the LLM, while
using the LLM for voter-level reasoning.

\subsection{LLMs as synthetic respondents and memory-conditioned
agents}\label{subsec:synthetic-respondents}

LLMs have also been studied as synthetic respondents or simulated human
samples. This literature is relevant because Synthetic Turkey does not only ask
agents to choose a candidate; it asks them to reason from demographic,
ideological, affective, and historical context. Argyle et al. propose the use
of LLMs as ``silicon samples'' for simulating human survey responses, while
Aher et al. test whether LLMs can replicate findings from human-subject
experiments \cite{argyle2023out,aher2023simulating}. Hwang et al. show that
demographics and ideology alone are insufficient to predict user opinions, and
that adding relevant past opinions improves prediction accuracy in
public-opinion tasks \cite{hwang2023aligning}. This supports the
methodological choice to give voter agents prior political memory and evolving
state variables rather than relying only on static archetype labels.

At the same time, LLM use in computational social science requires caution.
Ziems et al. evaluate 13 LLMs across 25 computational social science benchmarks
and conclude that LLMs can augment social-science workflows, especially in
annotation and explanation tasks, but their performance remains task-dependent
and does not remove the need for careful validation \cite{ziems2024css}.
Therefore, Synthetic Turkey is framed as a diagnostic simulation rather than
as a validated substitute for polling or survey-based electoral research.

\subsection{LLM-based political and electoral simulation}\label{subsec:electoral-sim}

A growing body of work applies LLM agents directly to political or electoral
settings. FlockVote uses LLM agents with demographic profiles and dynamic
contextual information to simulate the 2024 U.S.\ presidential election
\cite{zhou2025flockvote}. ElectionSim represents a more data-intensive
approach, building a million-level voter pool from social media and reporting
strong state-level performance in U.S.\ presidential election simulation
\cite{zhang2024electionsim}.

However, the Turkish case differs from these U.S.-centred examples. The 2023
Turkish presidential election involved a two-round presidential system, a
polarized and multi-party opposition bloc, Kurdish strategic voting, nationalist
protest voting, refugee politics, earthquake-related retrospective evaluation,
and a competitive-authoritarian institutional context. Comparative non-U.S.\
political simulation studies are therefore especially important, but the Turkish
case adds a different set of challenges: a two-round presidential system,
opposition coalition coordination, Kurdish strategic voting, nationalist protest
candidacy, and post-earthquake retrospective evaluation. Kang's Korean-language
electoral simulation identifies systematic failure modes, including progressive
bias, regional-polarization collapse, and third-party salience collapse
\cite{kang2026dynamok}. Kreutner et al.\ show that persona-driven LLM simulation
can approximate European Parliament voting behaviour, but also evaluate prompt
stability, counterfactual sensitivity, and generation-method effects
\cite{kreutner2026ep}. These studies support evaluating Synthetic Turkey not
only by final winner accuracy, but also by coalition structure, archetype-level
behaviour, aggregation sensitivity, and failure modes.

\subsection{Validity risks: bias, calibration, and
aggregation}\label{subsec:validity-risks}

The central methodological risk in LLM electoral simulation is that plausible
outputs may not correspond to calibrated behavioural probabilities.
Political-bias studies show that LLMs can exhibit systematic ideological
alignment patterns \cite{rettenberger2025politicalbias}. Public-opinion
simulation research further shows that LLM performance varies across countries,
demographic groups, and topics, raising particular concerns for non-Western and
non-English contexts \cite{qu2024performance}. Comparative election-simulation
studies also show that apparent winner accuracy can conceal vote-share errors
and third-party collapse \cite{kang2026dynamok}.

These concerns are central to this paper's evaluation strategy. Synthetic
Turkey compares not only final vote shares but also aggregation rules,
archetype-level behaviour, coalition structures, and failure modes. In
particular, the divergence between mean-probability aggregation and mode-vote
aggregation is treated as a substantive methodological finding: LLM-generated
candidate probabilities should not be interpreted as calibrated vote
probabilities without repeated-run validation and external calibration.

\subsection{Turkey as a hard case for electoral
simulation}\label{subsec:turkey-hard-case}

The 2023 Turkish election provides a demanding test case for LLM-agent
simulation. Erdoğan won despite economic crisis, refugee tensions, democratic
backsliding, and criticism of the government response to the February 2023
earthquakes. Çarkoğlu argues that Erdoğan's 2023 electoral survival cannot be
explained by economic concerns alone. Instead, vote choice was also shaped by
performance evaluations in areas such as security, foreign policy, and
conservative sensitivities, together with satisfaction with the way Turkish
democracy/regime works and long-standing identity cleavages around the Kurdish
question and the secularist/pro-Islamist divide \cite{carkoglu2024unlikely}. He
further finds that Kurdish and Alevi voters were negatively predisposed toward
Erdoğan, whereas support for Erdoğan was more likely among right-of-centre
voters and Sunni voters with higher levels of religious worship
\cite{carkoglu2024unlikely}.

Affective polarization further structured the electorate. Aytaç et al.\ find
that affective polarization was especially high among AKP and HDP supporters
and that polarization bolstered AKP voter loyalty in the 2023 election
\cite{aytac2025polarised}. This supports modelling affect and partisan loyalty
as central state variables rather than treating voters as purely economic or
spatial-policy actors.

The opposition side was also shaped by candidate-choice and coalition dilemmas.
Baruh and Çarkoğlu show that Kılıçdaroğlu was less appealing to some İYİ Party
and centre-right constituencies than alternatives such as İmamoğlu or Yavaş,
but he had a likeability advantage among voters with affinity toward HDP
\cite{baruh2024difficult}. This helps explain why pro-Kurdish voters could
strategically coordinate behind Kılıçdaroğlu without being modelled as ordinary
CHP voters. The same strategic coordination was constrained by securitization
and nationalist backlash risks \cite{ilbiz2023securitization}. Finally,
earthquake-zone voting cannot be reduced to automatic incumbent punishment:
Sevinç and Göz argue that earthquake-affected voters engaged in mediated
retrospective voting, evaluating the current government not only against its
2023 disaster response but against the perceived instability of the 1999
coalition era, making automatic incumbent punishment far from guaranteed
\cite{sevinc2024afet}.

\section{Methods}\label{sec:methods}
% =========================================================================

\subsection{Overview of the framework}\label{subsec:overview}

\textit{Synthetic Turkey} is a longitudinal generative agent-based model of the
2023 Turkish presidential election. It is generative in the specific sense that
voter-level reasoning and state updates are produced by an LLM, while the
broader simulation environment remains deterministic and source-controlled. The
simulation begins from a June 2018 post-election baseline and ends after the
28 May 2023 runoff result reveal. The completed baseline run contains 300 synthetic voter agents, 37 election-safe ticks, and 11{,}100 voter-decision calls.

Figure~\ref{fig:framework} summarizes the framework. The first stage constructs
a source-grounded population from configuration files: voter archetypes, 2018
baseline profiles, political actors, event timeline, credibility matrix, and
sampling profiles. The second stage turns these configurations into individual
voter souls. The third stage samples a 300-agent synthetic population. During
the simulation stage, each soul becomes a citizen agent with persistent beliefs,
affective state, episodic memory, and access to an LLM provider. At each event
tick, the system loads the relevant event, generates or retrieves controlled
political broadcast inputs, filters those inputs according to each voter's media
diet and credibility matrix, asks the voter LLM to reason, and stores updated
memory and belief states.

\subsection{Source-grounded voter archetype
construction}\label{subsec:archetypes}

The synthetic electorate was designed as a source-grounded diagnostic
population rather than a statistically representative micro-sample. Each agent
belongs to one of twelve voter archetypes intended to capture major political
blocs and behavioural pathways in the 2023 Turkish presidential election. The
goal was not to reproduce the full Turkish electorate at the individual level,
but to test whether an LLM-agent system can reproduce broad coalition
structures, identify sensitivity to aggregation rules, and reveal interpretable
failure modes.

The Erdoğan-aligned conservative archetypes were grounded in evidence that the
2023 Erdoğan vote was not driven by economic evaluations alone. Çarkoğlu shows
that Erdoğan's support persisted despite negative economic conditions because
conservative moral values, security, foreign policy, religiosity, right-wing
identity, and regime satisfaction remained important \cite{carkoglu2024unlikely}.
This supports separating strongly loyal conservative agents from economically
dissatisfied but culturally anchored conservative agents.

Affective polarization was included as a state variable because recent evidence
shows that Turkish voters were highly affectively polarized ahead of the 2023
election, especially AKP and HDP supporters. Aytaç et al.\ further show that
affective polarization reinforced AKP voter loyalty \cite{aytac2025polarised}.
Accordingly, highly polarized agents were modelled with lower cross-bloc
switching and stronger turnout resilience.

The Kurdish Political Voter archetype was modelled as a strategic anti-incumbent
voter rather than as a conventional CHP voter. Baruh and Çarkoğlu show that
Kılıçdaroğlu had a comparative advantage among HDP-affinity voters, while
nationalist-coded alternatives such as Mansur Yavaş carried a stronger penalty
among this constituency \cite{baruh2024difficult}. This supports a
candidate-utility structure in which Kurdish-aligned agents evaluate
Kılıçdaroğlu through democratic-restoration expectations, anti-incumbent
viability, and nationalist-penalty avoidance.

The Earthquake Zone Loyalist archetype was grounded in mediated retrospective
voting. Sevinç and Göz argue that earthquake-affected voters did not
mechanically punish the incumbent; instead, they engaged in a historically
comparative retrospective evaluation, weighing the current government's
response against the political instability of the 1999 coalition era
\cite{sevinc2024afet}. Therefore, disaster exposure was not modelled as an
automatic opposition shift.

Political broadcasts were modelled as affective and partisan inputs rather than
neutral information packets. Zeynel and Savut's analysis of the most-watched
campaign advertisements of the six highest-vote parties in the 2023 election
shows that emotional messaging differed according to party ideology and
ruling/opposition position \cite{zeynel2024duygular}. This supports including
affective framing in the event and broadcast design.

\subsection{Synthetic electorate construction}\label{subsec:electorate}

The electorate consists of 300 synthetic agents distributed across 12 voter
archetypes. These archetypes are designed to represent politically meaningful
blocs in Turkish electoral politics rather than statistically representative
survey clusters. The 12 archetypes are: Devout Anatolian Loyalist, Secular
Urban Professional, Conservative Economically Disillusioned, Alevi-CHP
Loyalist, Kurdish Political Voter, Nationalist Grey Wolf, Moderate Nationalist
İYİ, Pious Disillusioned Islamist, Young Urban Protest Voter, Earthquake Zone
Loyalist, Retired Protest Voter, and Cosmopolitan Liberal.

Each agent contains structured demographic and political information: age,
gender, city, region, education, income, employment, biography, worldview,
media diet, emotional baseline, behavioural tendencies, 2018 party and
presidential memory, and initial candidate probability distributions. The 2018
baseline is used as persona grounding and memory, not as a deterministic rule
for the 2023 vote. Agents are therefore allowed to change their preferences over
time through LLM reasoning.

The 300-agent sampling frame is anchored in the 2018 Turkish electoral baseline.
Party anchors are distributed as AKP 128 agents, CHP 68, HDP/DEM 35, MHP 33,
İYİ 30, and other small parties 6. Presidential anchors are distributed as
Erdoğan 158, Muharrem İnce 92, Selahattin Demirtaş 25, Meral Akşener 22,
Temel Karamollaoğlu 3, and Doğu Perinçek 0. The final archetype counts are
shown in Table~\ref{tab:electorate}.

The population should not be interpreted as a statistically representative
micro-sample of Turkish voters. The ideal design would use licensed survey
microdata and official demographic microdata. In this project, detailed
individual-level or highly granular cross-tabulated datasets were not available
as open research data. The personas are therefore research-informed synthetic
archetypes, supported by secondary research synthesis and encoded into local
JSON/YAML source files. This provides sociopolitical plausibility but limits
claims of statistical representativeness.

\subsection{Event timeline and election-safe ticks}\label{subsec:timeline}

The simulation exposes agents to a longitudinal 2018--2023 event timeline.
Events cover major political and social developments between the 2018 baseline
and the 2023 runoff. These include currency and inflation shocks, the 2019
Istanbul mayoral election, opposition alliance formation, Kurdish political
developments, campaign events, polling shifts, the February 2023 earthquakes,
first-round voting, runoff campaigning, and final result reveal.

A key design requirement is prevention of election-result leakage. The event
timeline contains actual 2023 election results, but voters must decide before
seeing those results. The engine therefore splits election events into decision
and reveal phases. For the first round, voters decide at
\texttt{T030A\_first\_round\_vote\_decision}; only after decisions are recorded
does \texttt{T030B\_first\_round\_result\_revealed} reveal the result. For the
runoff, voters decide at \texttt{T035A\_runoff\_vote\_decision}; only afterward
does \texttt{T035B\_final\_result\_revealed} reveal the final outcome. The
prompt also includes a temporal fence instructing the voter to use only
information available up to the simulated date.

\subsection{Political broadcasts and information
filtering}\label{subsec:broadcasts}

Political communication is represented as controlled broadcast inputs rather
than as fully autonomous strategic political agents. For each event tick, the
system identifies relevant political speakers and message frames from structured
configuration files. These message frames include actor identity, event-specific
framing, target groups, tone, and expected direction of influence. The important
methodological point is that these broadcasts do not mechanically shift votes.
They are visible inputs to the voter LLM, which then decides whether the message
is persuasive, ignored, reinforcing, mobilizing, or backfiring.

Each voter sees only a filtered subset of broadcasts. Filtering depends on media
diet, political credibility, event date, and relevant source. For example, a
pro-government loyalist and an opposition urban voter may receive different
visible messages from the same event because their media exposure and credibility
scores differ. This filtering layer is intended to make the information
environment more realistic than a single common national news feed.

The completed baseline run should therefore be described carefully: voter
decisions are LLM-first, but political broadcasts are controlled source-grounded
inputs. Future versions may make political actors more autonomous LLM agents,
but that is not the claim evaluated in this paper.

\subsection{Voter prompt and LLM decision loop}\label{subsec:prompt}

At each tick, each voter agent receives a prompt containing the following
components: temporal fence, role instruction, identity, archetype, demographic
profile, biography, political identity summary, worldview, media diet, social
context, 2018 baseline memory, current beliefs, current affective state, recent
episodic memories, current event, visible broadcasts, limited peer reflections,
and a strict JSON output schema.

The model is instructed to role-play as the synthetic voter rather than as a
political analyst. It must return structured values for government approval,
institutional trust, opposition trust, anger, fear, hope, sadness, political
fatigue, party preference, first-round vote intention, runoff vote intention,
turnout probability, reason codes, confidence, and a short first-person
reflection. Deterministic code validates the JSON, clamps numerical values to
valid ranges, normalizes probability distributions, and stores the updated
belief and memory state. It does not select the vote for the agent.

The completed run used \texttt{gpt-4o-mini} with temperature 0.45 and a maximum
output budget of 900 tokens per voter-decision call. The baseline run was
executed in May 2026. The orchestration code was implemented in Python 3.11,
using the OpenAI API for LLM calls, PyYAML for configuration loading, and
pandas/numpy for analysis. Parallel voter calls were executed through Python's
\texttt{concurrent.futures.ThreadPoolExecutor}. Soul sampling was deterministic,
using a fixed random seed of 20230528. Trajectory data were exported as a
long-format CSV table. Per-agent belief states, episodic memories, broadcast
logs, reflection logs, and the canonical run log were stored as JSON or JSON
Lines files.

All voter calls used the same system prompt template, with event-specific and
agent-specific fields filled deterministically from configuration files. If the
LLM response failed JSON parsing or omitted required fields, the call was
retried up to three times with linear backoff and a clarifying instruction
requiring strict JSON output. Successfully parsed responses were passed through
deterministic schema validation: numerical state variables were clamped to their
allowed scales, candidate probability distributions were normalized to sum to
one over canonical key sets, and the \texttt{confidence} and
\texttt{reason\_codes} fields were sanitized to their allowed types. If a call
still failed after retries, the engine could continue with a low-confidence
fallback that preserved the agent's previous belief and affect state and marked
the case as a provider error. In the baseline run reported here, this fallback
was never triggered. Because the temperature is non-zero and the model is
accessed through a hosted API, the run is a single stochastic realization rather
than a deterministic artifact, and exact byte-level replication depends on
provider-side model versioning.

\paragraph{Resource constraint and run protocol.}
A full 300-agent, 37-tick simulation produces 11{,}100 voter-decision LLM calls
plus political-broadcast generation calls. Because the run requires a large
number of hosted LLM calls, the paper reports one fully audited baseline run
rather than repeated full replications. This constraint is methodologically
important: every aggregate quantity reported here is a single sample from a
stochastic process at a fixed synthetic-population seed, and LLM run-to-run
variance cannot be estimated from one realization. Additional execution details
are reported in Appendix~\ref{app:protocol}. Future work should repeat the full
run across model versions, temperatures, prompt variants, and population seeds.

\paragraph{Use of generative AI in simulation and manuscript preparation.}
Large language models formed the core reasoning mechanism of the simulation.
In the completed run, \texttt{gpt-4o-mini} generated all structured voter
decisions and reflections from controlled prompts, as described above. Generative
AI tools were additionally used to support drafting and editing of this
manuscript. The author reviewed all generated content and takes responsibility
for the final text.

\subsection{Aggregation and scoring rules}\label{subsec:aggregation}

The simulation produces candidate probability distributions for each agent.
These probabilities can be aggregated in at least two reasonable ways.

The first rule is \textit{mean-probability aggregation}. For candidate $c$ and
agents $i=1,\ldots,N$, the simulated vote share is:
\begin{equation}
S_c^{\text{mean}} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}p_{ic},
\end{equation}
where $p_{ic}$ is agent $i$'s probability assigned to candidate $c$. This rule
treats each agent's probability distribution as fractional electoral support.

The second rule is \textit{mode-vote aggregation}. Let $T_i$ denote the set of
candidates or outcomes tied for agent $i$'s highest probability:
\begin{equation}
T_i = \{c \in C : p_{ic} = \max_{k \in C} p_{ik}\}.
\end{equation}
A tie-handling rule maps $T_i$ to one discrete ballot choice:
\begin{equation}
v_i = \tau(T_i).
\end{equation}
Candidate shares are then computed from the count of these discrete choices:
\begin{equation}
S_c^{\text{mode}} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\mathbb{1}(v_i=c).
\end{equation}

In the baseline analysis, the tie-handling function $\tau$ is evaluated under
three rules: the canonical candidate order used by the implementation, the
reverse candidate order, and Monte Carlo random tie-breaking.

These rules capture different interpretations of LLM-generated probabilities.
Mean-probability aggregation evaluates average probabilistic support, while
mode-vote aggregation approximates a ballot-like decision in which each voter
ultimately chooses one option after an explicit tie-handling rule is applied.

Aggregation was performed only after all voter-decision calls for the relevant
election tick were completed and validated. Mean-probability aggregation used the
normalized candidate probability distributions stored after validation. Mode-vote
aggregation assigned each agent to the candidate with the highest normalized
probability in their \texttt{first\_round\_vote\_intention} or
\texttt{runoff\_vote\_intention} distribution.

A non-trivial number of agents produced tied top probabilities. In the baseline
run, 50 of 300 first-round agents (16.7\%) and 54 of 300 runoff agents (18.0\%)
had two or more outcomes tied at the maximum probability. These ties were
typically cases where the LLM expressed uncertainty by assigning equal
probability to multiple outcomes. The primary implementation resolved ties
deterministically using the canonical candidate order in the aggregation code.
For the first round, this order was Erdoğan, Kılıçdaroğlu, Sinan Oğan,
Muharrem İnce, Other, and Undecided. For the runoff, this order was Erdoğan,
Kılıçdaroğlu, and Abstain/Invalid/Undecided. This ordering favours Erdoğan when
Erdoğan is part of a top-probability tie. Therefore, this paper reports mode-vote
outcomes under the canonical rule, a reverse-order rule, and a Monte Carlo
random tie-break rule.

The difference between aggregation rules and the sensitivity to tie handling are
central to the results.

\subsection{Evaluation targets}\label{subsec:evaluation}

The main external comparison target is the official 2023 Turkish presidential
election result. The first round is evaluated against Erdoğan 49.52\%,
Kılıçdaroğlu 44.88\%, Sinan Oğan 5.17\%, and Muharrem İnce 0.43\%. The runoff
is evaluated against Erdoğan 52.18\% and Kılıçdaroğlu 47.82\% \cite{ysk2023}.

Evaluation is performed at five levels: aggregate candidate shares,
scoring-rule sensitivity, archetype-level vote patterns, demographic
breakdowns, and longitudinal trajectories of approval and trust. This
multi-level evaluation is necessary because a simulation may fail at aggregate
prediction while still reproducing meaningful bloc-level structure, or may match
the winner while doing so for the wrong reasons.

\section{Results}\label{sec:results}

This section reports five layers of evaluation: aggregate vote shares
(Section~\ref{subsec:aggregate}), archetype-level patterns

\subsection{Aggregate outcome: aggregation and tie-handling
sensitivity}\label{subsec:aggregate}

The completed simulation should be read as a diagnostic realization rather than
as a prediction. The model produces internally coherent bloc-level behaviour,
but the aggregate winner depends on how probabilistic voter intentions are
converted into election-level outcomes. This distinction is central to the
evaluation: under mean-probability aggregation, the model overestimates
opposition consolidation and produces a Kılıçdaroğlu plurality; under the
implementation's canonical deterministic mode-vote rule, the same agents produce
an Erdoğan victory in both rounds. However, this mode-vote result is not robust
to tie handling. Because 16.7\% of first-round agents and 18.0\% of runoff
agents produce tied top probabilities, reversing the tie order or randomly
resolving tied choices changes the mode-vote interpretation.

Figure~\ref{fig:scoring} reports the first-round and runoff outcomes under
mean-probability aggregation and the canonical mode-vote rule. In the first
round, the actual result was Erdoğan 49.5\%, Kılıçdaroğlu 44.9\%, Oğan 5.2\%,
and İnce 0.4\%. Mean-probability aggregation gives Erdoğan 41.5\%,
Kılıçdaroğlu 49.1\%, and Oğan 1.4\%, producing a Kılıçdaroğlu plurality. The
canonical mode-vote rule gives Erdoğan 54.7\% and Kılıçdaroğlu 45.3\%,
identifying Erdoğan as the winner under that specific tie-breaking assumption,
but misses Oğan as a discrete top choice.

In the runoff, the actual result was Erdoğan 52.2\% and Kılıçdaroğlu 47.8\%.
Mean-probability aggregation gives Erdoğan 41.8\% and Kılıçdaroğlu 49.1\%,
with a substantial abstain/undecided component. The canonical mode-vote rule
gives Erdoğan 55.3\% and Kılıçdaroğlu 44.3\%.

Table~\ref{tab:voteshares} summarizes the same result numerically. The model
is not simply ``right'' or ``wrong''. It is winner-accurate under the canonical
deterministic mode-vote rule and winner-inaccurate under mean-probability
aggregation. However, Table~\ref{tab:tiehandling} shows that this canonical
mode-vote finding is tie-break-sensitive. Under the reverse-order rule, the
first-round mode-vote result shifts to Kılıçdaroğlu 46.67\% and Erdoğan
41.00\%, with 11.67\% assigned to Undecided. Under random tie-breaking,
Kılıçdaroğlu has a narrow mean advantage in the first round, 48.34\% versus
Erdoğan's 46.56\%, with overlapping uncertainty intervals. The runoff shows a
similar pattern: the canonical rule gives Erdoğan 55.33\%, but random
tie-breaking gives nearly indistinguishable mean shares for Erdoğan and
Kılıçdaroğlu. Thus, the simulation does not robustly identify the correct
winner. The apparent canonical mode-vote success is partly an artifact of
deterministic tie handling. This makes calibration, aggregation, and tie
handling central methodological issues for LLM-based synthetic electorates.

\subsection{Archetype-level voting: coalition structure is stronger than
aggregate prediction}\label{subsec:archetype}

Although the aggregate result is not robust across probability-to-vote
conversion rules, the archetype-level mode-vote results show that the simulation
captures several broad coalition structures. Figure~\ref{fig:archetypes} shows
first-round mode-vote by archetype.

\begin{figure*}[!htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{Fig3_archetype_mode_vote.png}
\caption{First-round mode-vote by archetype: share of agents in each archetype
whose top-ranked first-round candidate was the listed candidate.}
\label{fig:archetypes}
\end{figure*}

Several patterns are substantively plausible. The Devout Anatolian Loyalist,
Nationalist Grey Wolf, and Earthquake Zone Loyalist archetypes vote entirely for
Erdoğan. Secular Urban Professional, Alevi-CHP Loyalist, Young Urban Protest
Voter, and Cosmopolitan Liberal archetypes vote entirely for Kılıçdaroğlu.
Kurdish Political Voters also consolidate behind Kılıçdaroğlu, consistent with
strategic opposition voting. Conservative Economically Disillusioned and Pious
Disillusioned Islamist voters remain mostly Erdoğan-oriented but show some
erosion. Moderate Nationalist İYİ voters lean strongly toward Kılıçdaroğlu but
retain a pro-Erdoğan minority. Retired Protest Voters split evenly.

This result suggests that the model performs better at reproducing broad
coalition blocs than at representing internally fragmented or protest-oriented
behaviour. The clearest success is the reproduction of the Erdoğan loyalist core
and the opposition consolidation of secular, Kurdish, young urban, Alevi-CHP,
and cosmopolitan liberal blocs. The most important failure is the absence of a
discrete Oğan electorate. Although the simulation includes nationalist
archetypes, it does not include a sufficiently strong nationalist-protest or ATA
Alliance pathway capable of making Oğan the top first-round choice for a
meaningful number of agents.

The Earthquake Zone Loyalist archetype is especially important. A simple
retrospective-accountability model might expect the February 2023 earthquakes
to strongly punish the incumbent. The simulation instead preserves Erdoğan
loyalty in this archetype, matching the broader empirical puzzle that disaster
exposure did not automatically translate into opposition voting among all
affected conservative communities. This is a meaningful validation success at
the archetype level, even though it does not remove the aggregate calibration
problems.

\subsection{The Oğan miss: a representation gap rather than random
noise}\label{subsec:ogan}

The model underestimates Sinan Oğan's first-round support. The actual Oğan vote
share was 5.17\%, while the mean-probability simulation gives 1.37\% and the
mode-vote simulation gives 0\%. This is not merely a small numerical error. In
a 300-agent simulation, a 5.17\% vote share corresponds to roughly 15 agents.
The model produces no discrete bloc of agents for whom Oğan is the top
candidate.

This failure exposes a representation gap in the synthetic electorate. The
framework contains nationalist voters, especially the Nationalist Grey Wolf and
Moderate Nationalist İYİ archetypes. However, these archetypes are not enough
to represent the specific nationalist-protest pathway that made Oğan electorally
relevant in the first round. The Nationalist Grey Wolf archetype remains
overwhelmingly Erdoğan-oriented, while Moderate Nationalist İYİ voters mainly
consolidate behind Kılıçdaroğlu. As a result, nationalist fragmentation appears
as low-probability uncertainty rather than as a distinct ballot choice.

The tie analysis adds nuance to this failure. Oğan appears in a small number of
tied top-probability distributions, including two first-round ties among
Erdoğan, Oğan, and Undecided and one tie between Erdoğan and Oğan. However,
he almost never becomes a stable top-ranked candidate. The problem is therefore
not that the model assigns Oğan no salience at all; rather, it fails to convert
nationalist-protest uncertainty into a discrete Oğan-supporting bloc.

For future simulations, this suggests that protest-vote candidates cannot be
treated as residual probabilities attached to broader ideological blocs. They
may require dedicated archetypes, event sensitivities, and campaign-message
pathways. In the Turkish 2023 case, a dedicated ATA Alliance or
nationalist-protest archetype would likely be necessary
\cite{gerim2025victory,karaca2024socialmedia}. This interpretation is consistent
with non-English LLM election-simulation work showing that third-party salience
can collapse into major-bloc reasoning \cite{kang2026dynamok}.

\subsection{Demographic breakdowns: coherent but not independent
validation}\label{subsec:demographic}

Figure~\ref{fig:demographics} reports demographic breakdowns of first-round
mode-vote by region, education, age bracket, and city. These outputs should be
interpreted as diagnostic checks rather than independent survey estimates.
Because demographics are generated through the archetype sampling process,
demographic gradients partly reflect the design of the synthetic population.
They should therefore not be read as causal estimates of education, age, city,
or region effects.

\begin{figure*}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{Fig5_demographic_breakdowns.png}
\caption{Demographic breakdown of first-round mode-vote: by region (top left),
education (top right), age bracket (bottom left), and city (bottom right, top
ten by N).}
\label{fig:demographics}
\end{figure*}

Despite that limitation, the breakdowns are useful for checking whether the
synthetic electorate produces plausible cleavages. Urban and western contexts
such as Marmara, the Aegean, Istanbul, Ankara, and Izmir lean toward
Kılıçdaroğlu. Central Anatolia, the Black Sea, Kayseri, Konya, Sivas, Erzurum,
Yozgat, and Mersin show stronger Erdoğan dominance in the sampled population.
Younger voters lean strongly toward Kılıçdaroğlu, while older groups are more
Erdoğan-oriented. Graduate and university-student categories are fully
Kılıçdaroğlu in the simulation, while primary, high-school, and vocational
groups lean more Erdoğan.

These gradients are directionally coherent, but they also reveal over-determinism.
Several groups are reported as 100\% for one candidate, and some city-level
groups have small sample sizes. The demographic outputs are therefore best used
to diagnose internal consistency, not to claim that the model independently
validates Turkish demographic voting patterns.

\subsection{Longitudinal trajectories: the damping
problem}\label{subsec:longitudinal}

The longitudinal trust and approval trajectories show a second important
diagnostic pattern. Figure~\ref{fig:trajectories} plots mean government
approval, institutional trust, and opposition trust across simulation ticks.
The model captures the direction of political movement: government approval and
institutional trust decline around the earthquake period, while opposition trust
rises modestly. However, the magnitude of numerical movement is small.

\begin{figure*}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{Fig4_trust_trajectories.png}
\caption{Mean government approval, institutional trust, and opposition trust
across 37 simulation ticks. Background bands mark major event clusters for
orientation rather than causal attribution.}
\label{fig:trajectories}
\end{figure*}

This is the \textit{damping problem}: the LLM produces event-aware reflections
and plausible qualitative reasoning, but the numerical state variables move less
than expected. In other words, the agents often narrate political change more
strongly than they encode it in approval, trust, or emotion scores. This matters
because longitudinal simulation depends not only on final vote choice but also
on state dynamics. If trust and affect remain too stable, the model may
understate the cumulative effect of crises, campaigns, and institutional shocks.

The damping problem is not only a limitation; it is a useful diagnostic finding.
It suggests that future LLM-agent simulations should evaluate the alignment
between natural-language reflection and numerical state updates. A model may
appear politically responsive in text while remaining numerically inert. In this
run, the qualitative narratives were more event-sensitive than the structured
numerical state variables.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}
% =========================================================================

\subsection{Aggregation sensitivity as a validity
problem}\label{subsec:agg-sensitivity}

The central result is that the same LLM-agent run can support different election
interpretations depending on the probability-to-vote conversion rule.
Mean-probability aggregation produces a Kılıçdaroğlu plurality. Canonical
mode-vote aggregation produces an Erdoğan victory, but this result is sensitive
to the deterministic tie-break rule used by the implementation. When tied top
probabilities are resolved by reverse ordering or random tie-breaking, the
mode-vote result shifts toward Kılıçdaroğlu or becomes statistically ambiguous.

This matters because real elections count discrete ballots, but LLM agents
often return probability distributions rather than discrete choices. Converting
those distributions into votes is therefore not a neutral technical step.
Mean-probability aggregation treats uncertainty as fractional electoral support.
Mode-vote aggregation approximates a ballot-like decision, but tied top
probabilities require an additional tie-handling rule. In this simulation, a
sizeable minority of agents produced such ties. The main methodological lesson
is therefore broader than the original mean-versus-mode contrast: LLM electoral
simulations require explicit reporting of probability calibration,
discrete-vote conversion, and tie-handling assumptions.

This finding also reframes the apparent winner result. The canonical mode-vote
rule is closer to the mechanics of a discrete ballot than mean-probability
aggregation, but the canonical Erdoğan victory should not be interpreted as
robust predictive success because it depends on deterministic tie ordering. The
safer interpretation is that the simulation captures some bloc-level structure
while exposing instability in the probability-to-vote conversion layer.

\subsection{Bloc-level plausibility versus individual-level
representativeness}\label{subsec:bloc}

At the bloc level, the model performs better than it does as an aggregate
forecaster. It reproduces the loyalist Erdoğan core, secular opposition
consolidation, Kurdish strategic support for Kılıçdaroğlu, and the persistence
of earthquake-zone loyalism. These patterns are consistent with the literature
used to ground the archetypes: Erdoğan's 2023 resilience was linked not only to
economic evaluations but also to conservative identity, security, foreign policy,
religiosity, and regime satisfaction \cite{carkoglu2024unlikely}; affective
polarization reinforced partisan loyalty, especially among AKP supporters
\cite{aytac2025polarised}; and Kılıçdaroğlu held a comparative advantage among
HDP-affinity voters relative to more nationalist-coded opposition alternatives
\cite{baruh2024difficult}.

However, bloc-level plausibility is not the same as individual-level
representativeness. The agents are source-grounded archetypes rather than
sampled respondents from a representative survey. Consequently, the model can
be useful for testing whether broad political pathways are encoded coherently,
but it cannot estimate real voter probabilities or subgroup vote shares with
survey-like validity. The demographic outputs should therefore be read as
internal diagnostics of the synthetic population rather than independent
evidence about Turkish voters.

\subsection{Third-party and protest-vote collapse}\label{subsec:thirdparty}

The clearest substantive failure is the underproduction of Sinan Oğan support.
The model contains nationalist archetypes, but it does not create a discrete
first-round nationalist-protest bloc. Instead, Oğan appears mainly as residual
probability inside broader nationalist or right-wing categories. This suggests
that protest-vote candidates require explicit representation in the population
design, event salience, and candidate-utility logic. In the Turkish case, an ATA
Alliance or anti-immigration nationalist-protest archetype would likely be
necessary.

This failure is methodologically important because it resembles third-party
salience collapse identified in another non-English LLM electoral simulation
\cite{kang2026dynamok}. It suggests that LLM-agent electorates may reproduce
dominant coalition blocs more easily than smaller, strategically meaningful
third-party pathways. Future LLM electoral simulations should therefore report
not only final winner accuracy but also whether third candidates, protest blocs,
and abstention pathways are represented at realistic magnitudes.

\subsection{Implications for LLM-agent electoral
simulation}\label{subsec:implications}

The findings show the value of diagnostic failure analysis. A conventional
election-prediction framing would classify the model as either correct or
incorrect. That would miss the more interesting methodological result: the
simulation is bloc-plausible at the archetype level, unstable under aggregation
and tie-handling choices, weak on protest-nationalist representation, and damped
in numerical state trajectories. This mixed profile is exactly why LLM-agent
simulations should be evaluated at multiple levels rather than only by final
aggregate accuracy.

For future work, the minimum reporting standard for LLM-agent election
simulations should include: the population construction method, prompt and
schema design, event chronology and leakage controls, model identifier and
temperature, aggregation rules, tie-handling rules, repeated-run uncertainty
where feasible, and failure-mode analysis by political bloc. Synthetic Turkey
contributes to this emerging standard by showing how a historically specific,
non-Western, multi-party election exposes validity problems that may remain
hidden in simpler winner-focused simulations.

\section{Limitations}\label{sec:limitations}
% =========================================================================

\subsection{Simulation robustness}\label{subsec:robustness}

The completed simulation is a single stochastic realization. The LLM was run at
temperature 0.45, so run-to-run variation is expected. The tie-handling Monte
Carlo analysis estimates uncertainty only for tied top-probability conversion
inside the completed run; it does not estimate full LLM run-to-run variance.
Repeated full simulations across model versions, temperatures, prompt variants,
and population seeds would be required to make stronger robustness claims.

The primary mode-vote result is also sensitive to tie handling. A sizeable
minority of agents produced tied top probabilities at the election decision
ticks. The deterministic candidate ordering used in the baseline aggregation
favours Erdoğan when Erdoğan is included in a top-probability tie. This does
not invalidate the comparison between aggregation rules, but it means that the
canonical mode-vote result should be interpreted as conditional on an explicit
tie-breaking assumption. Future work should report random tie-breaking,
candidate-order reversals, and probabilistic sampling from the candidate
distribution as robustness checks.

\subsection{Population grounding and
representativeness}\label{subsec:population}

The synthetic electorate is not generated from licensed survey microdata or
official individual-level demographic data. The voter archetypes are
research-informed and source-grounded, but they remain synthetic constructs.
This limits claims of statistical representativeness. Subgroup analysis is also
limited by sample size: a 300-agent population is useful for a small-scale
diagnostic simulation, but some city, education, and archetype subgroups are
small. Their percentages should be read cautiously.

The model also simplifies within-archetype heterogeneity. Kurdish, Alevi, İYİ,
conservative-disillusioned, young protest, and retired voters are internally
diverse. The current design captures broad blocs but may flatten intra-group
variation. The Retired Protest Voter archetype is especially exploratory because
direct literature support is weaker than for the Kurdish, Erdoğan-loyalist,
earthquake-zone, or nationalist-protest pathways.

\subsection{LLM validity and contamination}\label{subsec:llmvalidity}

LLM contamination is a serious concern. The model may have latent knowledge of
the 2023 Turkish election from pretraining. The simulation uses temporal fencing
and election-safe decision ticks, but prompt-level fencing cannot fully remove
latent historical knowledge. The model may also encode uneven political knowledge
across countries, languages, and demographic groups, which is particularly
important in a non-Western and non-English case.

The model contains researcher priors. Archetype definitions, sampling weights,
credibility matrices, event descriptions, broadcast frames, and prompt wording
all shape the simulation. This is not unique to LLM simulations, but it must be
disclosed because these priors influence outputs. The reproducibility supplement
classifies input components by epistemic category, distinguishing empirical
inputs, synthetic attributes, heuristic rules, and LLM-generated outputs so
that the source of each modelling assumption can be audited.

\subsection{Political-behaviour and causal-inference
limits}\label{subsec:political}

The political broadcast layer in the completed baseline run is controlled rather
than fully autonomous. Political actors are represented through structured
broadcast inputs and filtered visibility, not as independent strategic LLM
agents continuously optimizing campaign behaviour. The model also lacks a
dedicated nationalist-protest pathway for Sinan Oğan, producing a clear
underestimation of Oğan support and showing that small but politically
consequential blocs can disappear when they are represented only as residual
probability inside broader archetypes.

Causal event attribution is limited. Because there is only one run and no
ablation design, the paper cannot claim that a specific event caused a specific
vote shift. Event interpretation is descriptive and diagnostic rather than
causal. Numerical state trajectories are also damped: trust, approval, and
affect move in the expected direction around major events, but the magnitude is
small. This raises concern that the LLM is more responsive in reflection text
than in structured numerical updates.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}
% =========================================================================

This paper presented \textit{Synthetic Turkey}, a source-grounded generative
agent-based simulation of the 2023 Turkish presidential election. The study does
not demonstrate that LLM agents can reliably predict elections. Instead, it
shows that a controlled LLM-mediated synthetic electorate can reproduce some
coalition-level structures while exposing clear failure modes in aggregation,
calibration, archetype coverage, and longitudinal state dynamics.

The most important finding is that probability-to-vote conversion rules
materially shape the simulated outcome. Mean-probability aggregation produces a
Kılıçdaroğlu plurality, while canonical mode-vote aggregation produces an
Erdoğan victory. However, the canonical mode-vote result is sensitive to
top-probability tie handling: under alternative tie rules, the simulated winner
shifts toward Kılıçdaroğlu or becomes ambiguous. This result shows that
LLM-generated voter probabilities cannot be interpreted naively. The conversion
from probabilistic intention to electoral outcome is itself a modelling choice.

The simulation is strongest at reproducing broad political blocs: loyalist
Erdoğan voters, secular opposition voters, Kurdish strategic opposition voters,
and earthquake-zone loyalists. It is weakest at representing protest-nationalist
fragmentation, especially the Oğan vote. It also shows over-homogenization in
some opposition archetypes and damped numerical movement in trust and affect.

The broader implication is that LLM-agent electoral simulations should be
evaluated as diagnostic instruments before they are treated as predictive
models. Their value lies not only in whether they match an election result, but
in where they fail, which assumptions create those failures, and how those
failures reveal the structure of the model.

\section{Summary of scoring-rule interpretation}\label{app:scoring}

Mean-probability aggregation is useful when the research question concerns
average probabilistic support. However, it can be misleading if LLM
probabilities are not calibrated. Mode-vote aggregation is closer to the
mechanics of a discrete ballot, but it discards uncertainty and requires an
explicit tie-handling rule when two or more outcomes share the maximum
probability. The divergence between aggregation rules and tie-handling rules in
this study suggests that future LLM electoral simulations should report
mean-probability shares, discrete mode-vote shares, and tie-handling
sensitivity.

\section{Computational run protocol}\label{app:protocol}

The May 2026 baseline run required approximately 8--10 hours of wall-clock
execution at the chosen concurrency level and incurred approximately 8--10 USD
in API cost at the pricing available during the run. These values are reported
only to document the resource constraint that motivated the single-run design;
they are not expected to be stable across providers, model versions, or future
pricing schedules.

\section{Recommended future extensions}\label{app:extensions}

Future work should include repeated simulation runs, mock-versus-LLM ablations,
explicit calibration tests, a dedicated nationalist-protest archetype, stronger
modelling of Oğan-like third-candidate pathways, improved numerical state-update
prompting, and more empirically grounded demographic sampling. Political
broadcast generation could also be extended toward autonomous but constrained
LLM political agents, while preserving source control and leakage prevention.

\section{Prompt and output-schema summary}\label{app:prompt}

Each voter prompt contained eight blocks: (1) temporal fence, (2) voter
identity and archetype, (3) demographic and political biography, (4) 2018
baseline memory, (5) current belief and affect state, (6) recent episodic
memories, (7) current event and visible political broadcasts, and (8) strict
JSON response schema. The model was instructed to answer as the voter, not as
an external political analyst.

The JSON schema required structured fields for government approval, institutional
trust, opposition trust, anger, fear, hope, sadness, political fatigue, turnout
probability, party preference, first-round vote intention, runoff vote intention,
candidate probability distribution, confidence, reason codes, and a short
first-person reflection. Numerical fields were bounded on fixed scales and
validated by deterministic code. Candidate probability distributions were
normalized after validation.

The full prompt template and JSON output schema are included in the
reproducibility supplement (Online Resource 1).

\section{Event timeline and leakage controls}\label{app:timeline}

The full event timeline is included in the reproducibility supplement
(Online Resource 1). In the main run, election events were separated into
decision and reveal phases. Agents made first-round decisions before the
first-round result reveal and runoff decisions before the final result reveal.
Each decision prompt included a temporal fence instructing the agent to reason
only from information available at the simulated date. This reduces but does not
eliminate the risk of latent historical knowledge in the underlying LLM.
